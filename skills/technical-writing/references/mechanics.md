# Words and Mechanics — The Surface Craft

The concrete sentence- and word-level do's and don'ts scattered through *The Sense
of Style*, concentrated in the curse-of-knowledge and classic-style chapters.

> **Master heuristic that subsumes everything below.** Every vice here is a vice
> because it breaks the window — it draws attention to the prose, the writer, or an
> abstraction instead of the thing being shown. Encode the *window* metaphor; treat
> these rules as its consequences, not a checklist.

Pinker's roster of "soggy prose" habits: *metadiscourse, signposting, hedging,
apologizing, professional narcissism, clichés, mixed metaphors, metaconcepts,
zombie nouns, and unnecessary passives.*

## 1. Nominalizations / zombie nouns

Nouns manufactured from verbs and adjectives by suffixes **-ation, -ment,
-ance/-ence, -ity, -ing, -tion, -ism**. "The nominalization rule takes a perfectly
spry verb and embalms it into a lifeless noun." They lumber across the scene with
no conscious agent — "zombie nouns."

Why they deaden prose: they hide the action (the verb is the sentence's engine),
often hide the agent, force in a weak light verb (*make, effect, conduct, perform,
achieve*), and add words. A hallmark of academese and a downstream symptom of
functional fixity.

**The fix — "reverbify":** turn the zombie noun back into its verb and restore the
agent.

| Zombie-noun version | Reverbified |
|---|---|
| make an appearance | appear |
| bring about the organization of | organize |
| effect the affirmation of an idea | affirm the idea |
| implement a postponement | postpone |
| reach a decision | decide |
| conduct an investigation of | investigate |
| place an emphasis on faith | emphasize faith |
| present a summary of X | summarize X |
| the cancellation of X | cancel X |
| the implementation of the policy led to delays | the policy delayed things |
| there was a reduction in prejudice | they reduced prejudice |
| the editorial process | editing |

> **The exception — do NOT blanket-ban.** A nominalization can be an economical way
> to refer to a topic you've just introduced. Once an action is on the table,
> naming it as a noun lets you make *it* the subject of the next sentence (good for
> given-before-new flow). Reverbify by default; keep the nominalization when it
> names an already-introduced topic so the next sentence can comment on it.

## 2. The passive voice (Pinker DEFENDS it)

The blanket "avoid the passive" rule is **bad advice.** The passive has
indispensable functions because of how it engages attention and memory. Active and
passive carry the *same content* but differ in **information order**, and order
drives comprehension. (Strunk & White even botched their own passive examples —
"There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground" is not passive.)

> **Decision rule.** Use the passive when **the affected entity is the topic of the
> preceding discussion**, OR when **the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or heavy** and
> best omitted or postponed.

Three legitimate uses:
1. **Given-before-new.** Put already-known info first (as subject), new info last.
   Honors "save the heaviest for last" and "given, then new."
2. **Agent unknown/irrelevant/omitted.** The passive (unlike the active) can drop
   the doer entirely: "the fires were put out by helicopters" — you needn't know a
   guy named Bob flew one.
3. **Postpone a heavy or old-news doer.** Move a grammatically complex agent to the
   end, relieving memory and keeping end-focus on what's new.

When the passive is **wrong** (red flags):
- **Evading responsibility** — "mistakes were made" to keep a name out of the
  spotlight. This abuse is distinct from the legitimate uses.
- **Breaking topic continuity** — if the active keeps the established topic in
  subject position and the passive would shove it aside, prefer active.
- **Reflexive, purposeless** passivity that just buries the actor and adds *be* +
  *by*.

> **Never auto-convert passive→active.** Check: is the subject the running topic? Is
> the agent unknown/irrelevant/heavy? If yes → passive is correct. If it merely
> hides a responsible agent or disrupts topic flow → rewrite active.

Justified (agent irrelevant): "The king was killed in battle."
Unjustified (evasion): "Errors were committed in the handling of the account." →
"We mishandled the account."

## 3. Metadiscourse and signposting

Metadiscourse = verbiage about verbiage (*subsection, review, this essay, to sum
up*). **Signposting** is its commonest subtype: "say what you'll say, say it, say
what you said" ("In the first section we will…", "As noted above…").

**Minimize, don't abolish.** When you must guide, prefer:
- **Summative / conversational** signposting ("As we have just seen…", "Now that we
  have X, let's…") over **advance roadmaps** ("In this section I will…"). The "we"
  framing makes it a shared journey, not a table of contents.
- **Open with a question** instead of announcing the topic — Pinker's flagship
  technique.

Before: *This chapter discusses the factors that cause names to rise and fall in
popularity.*
After: **What makes a name rise and fall in popularity?**

> Strip advance roadmaps. Allow sparse conversational summatives. Convert topic
> announcements into questions. Keep any signpost that genuinely prevents a reader
> getting lost in a long, complex stretch.

## 4. Hedging

"Compulsive hedging" — qualifiers added to dodge responsibility. Detector list:
*almost, apparently, in part, so to speak, somewhat, partially, presumably,
relatively, virtually, comparatively, fairly, rather, to a certain degree.* Motive
(per Pinker): academics hedge "to get off the hook should anyone ever try to prove
them wrong" — defensive CYA. It blurs the window.

**Intensifiers are disguised hedges** — *very, highly, extremely* pre-qualify a
statement that would otherwise stand as an absolute (see §8).

**The remedy is not deletion-by-rule.** "You can count on the common sense of
readers to fill in the missing hedges." Write with conviction.

> **Qualify, don't hedge.** Keep a qualifier that *changes the truth conditions*
> (real scope the reader needs); cut one that's just self-protection. Test: does the
> qualifier change what's true, or only shield the writer?

Before: *The results somewhat suggest that the approach may, in part, be fairly
effective in certain respects.*
After: *The approach works.* (or, if scope is real: *The approach works for X but
not Y.*)

## 5. Clichés, mixed metaphors, stock phrases

Canned formulas break the spell — "like an actor with a wooden delivery." Mixed
metaphors are doubly bad: stacked dead figures produce nonsense the reader
half-pictures ("the company's bread and butter had risen… coasting on its laurels…
a low-octane swan song… a renaissance man drilling down to core issues and pushing
the envelope").

**The rule is not a blacklist — freshen or twist.** Insist on fresh wording and
concrete imagery that keeps the reader's sensory cortex lit up. Even a cliché can be
made new by inverting it:
"Directing team owners is like herding cats." → "To suggest that directing team
owners is like herding cats is to give cats a bad name."

The deep source of the skill is *wide reading* — an absorbed inventory of words,
idioms, and a feel for how they mesh or clash. Operationally: detect stock phrasing,
then freshen or twist it rather than mechanically deleting.

## 6. Officialese — bloated → plain

Writers entomb actors and actions in **verbal coffins** — bloated multiword phrases.
Mantra: *omit needless words* (Pinker mocks "superfluous and unnecessary" as itself
redundant).

| Bloated / officialese | Plain |
|---|---|
| for the purpose of | to |
| in view of the fact that | since |
| in the eventuality that / in the event that | if |
| at this point in time / at the present time | now |
| in order to | to |
| due to the fact that / owing to the fact that | because |
| in the nature of | like |
| a large number of | many |
| a majority of | most |
| has the ability to / is able to | can |
| in the absence of | without |
| with regard to / with respect to | about, on |
| in spite of the fact that | although |
| subsequent to | after |
| prior to | before |
| in close proximity to | near |
| during the course of | during |
| make a decision regarding | decide |

> Treat as a search-and-replace table, but apply through the window heuristic —
> don't replace where the longer form is genuinely needed for register or rhythm.
>
> *(Fidelity note: "for the purpose of → to" and "in view of the fact that → since"
> are confirmed entries in Pinker's own table; the rest are standard plain-English
> substitutions fully consistent with his method.)*

## 7. Concrete diction and metaconcepts

Prefer the concrete, specific word; functional fixity makes experts default to
abstractions. A concrete "telling detail" can stand in for an abstraction and is
more memorable.

**Metaconcepts = concepts about concepts.** "Could you recognize a *level* or a
*perspective* if you met one on the street?" Detector list: *approach, assumption,
concept, condition, context, framework, issue, level, model, perspective, process,
range, role, strategy, tendency, variable.* They are "the packing material in which
academics and bureaucrats clad their subject matter."

| Metaconcept padding | Stripped |
|---|---|
| the editorial process | editing |
| a prejudice reduction model | reducing prejudice |
| on the aspirational level | aspire |
| approach this from a law-enforcement perspective | call the police |
| the implementation process for the framework | how we'll do it |

> Flag every metaconcept word; ask "what concrete act or object does this wrap?" and
> replace with that act or object.

## 8. Intensifiers and verbal tics

*Very, highly, extremely, really, actually, basically, quite* generally weaken
rather than strengthen — they pre-qualify what could be an absolute and dilute it.
Prefer one stronger word:
- "very big" → **huge**; "very smart" → **brilliant**; "really important" →
  **crucial**; "very fast" → **rapid**.
- "actually, basically, in fact, of course" as throat-clearing → **cut.**

## 9. Punctuation as a parsing aid

Pinker treats punctuation as a cognitive tool for parsing and grouping, "somewhat
subjective" but far from "entirely subjective."

- **Avoid garden paths.** Punctuation's job is to stop misparsing and backtracking.
  The **comma splice** is his most-hated error because it "always creates a garden
  path." Non-negotiable "I-am-illiterate" errors: run-on, comma splice, grocer's
  apostrophe, comma between subject and predicate, possessive *it's*.
- **Serial (Oxford) comma — use it** unless house style forbids; it's clearer for
  grouping.
- **Logical nesting of delimiters.** Decries putting commas/periods inside quotation
  marks regardless of sense: "aesthetic habit should be trumped by logic."
- **Em-dashes, colons, block quotations = attention tools.** "The deliberate use of
  surprising transitions — colons, dashes, block quotations — is one of the
  hallmarks of lively prose." Use dashes/colons to group a parenthetical or
  spotlight what follows.
- **Semicolon** — the most underrated mark; joins closely related independent
  clauses and fixes would-be comma splices.
- **Possessives by logic:** *Charles's son*, not *Charles' son*.

## Encoding cheat-sheet

| Vice | Detector | Default action | Keep-it exception |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zombie noun | -tion/-ment/-ance/-ity + light verb (make/effect/conduct) | Reverbify; restore agent | Naming an already-introduced topic |
| Passive | *be* + past participle (+ *by*) | **Don't auto-convert**; test info-flow | Given-before-new; agent unknown/irrelevant/heavy |
| Metadiscourse/signposting | "in this section", "to sum up", "as noted above" | Cut advance roadmaps; use questions / "we"-summative | Sparse guidance in long, complex passages |
| Hedging | somewhat, fairly, apparently, in part, presumably | Cut; trust the reader | When it carries real scope/precision |
| Cliché / mixed metaphor | stock idioms; stacked dead figures | Freshen, invert, or make concrete | Inverted/twisted cliché with poetic charge |
| Officialese coffin | "for the purpose of", "at this point in time" | Substitute plain phrase (table) | Register genuinely requires it |
| Metaconcept | level, perspective, framework, model, process, issue | Strip packaging; name the act/object | When the meta-level is the real subject |
| Intensifier/tic | very, really, actually, basically | Single vivid word, or cut | Genuine degree distinction |
| Parsing punctuation | comma splice, missing serial comma | Fix splice; serial comma; dash/colon for grouping | — |

> **Above all rules:** is the prose a clean window onto the thing — or has it fogged
> into talk about the text, the writer's caution, or an abstraction? Fix toward the
> window.
