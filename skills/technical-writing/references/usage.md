# Telling Right from Wrong — Usage

From Ch. 6. Verdicts framed for direct application: **IGNORE** (fake rule, don't
enforce), **KEEP** (real rule, flag it), **DEPENDS** (judgment by clarity +
audience).

## Pinker's core stance

He rejects the **sticklers' founding myth**: the false binary that *either* all
traditional rules must be obeyed *or* anything goes. There's no real
prescriptivist-vs-descriptivist war — it's as bogus as "nature vs. nurture."

His test for any rule, in priority order:
1. **Does it serve clarity, grace, or precision for the reader?** If it exists only
   to honor tradition, Latin, or a 19th-century peeve, discard it.
2. **What do skilled, careful writers actually do?** (consult the corpus, not your
   gut)
3. **What do usage authorities and data say?** Triangulate the American Heritage
   Usage Panel, Merriam-Webster's *Dictionary of English Usage*, the *Cambridge
   Grammar* — not one style manual treated as scripture.

**Real rule vs. "bubbe-meise"** (Yiddish: grandmother's tale — his term for a fake
rule; also *folklore, superstition, shibboleth*):

| A REAL rule… | A FAKE rule (bubbe-meise)… |
|---|---|
| Has a reason rooted in clarity/comprehension | Is justified by "tradition," Latin grammar, or pseudo-logic |
| Is observed by the best writers across centuries | Is violated constantly by the best writers (always has been) |
| When broken, produces genuine ambiguity | When broken, bothers only sticklers; meaning is clear |
| Survives "did Shakespeare/the KJV break it?" | Was invented by a named 18th–19th c. peever (Lowth, Dryden) |

**The escape hatch / audience principle.** Usage trivia is "the smallest part of
good writing" — it pales behind coherence, classic style, and beating the curse of
knowledge. But in formal or high-stakes contexts, *observing even a superstition*
can be worth it to avoid distracting a sticklerish reader. Know your audience.

## Myths / fake rules — IGNORE (don't flag the "violation")

| "Rule" | Verdict | Rationale | Example (allowed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Don't split infinitives | **IGNORE** | "Quintessential bogus rule," invented to ape Latin (where infinitives are one word and can't split). English splits naturally, often more clearly. | "to **boldly** go"; "to more than **double** sales" |
| Don't end a sentence with a preposition | **IGNORE** | Completely invented (Dryden's Latin-worship). Contorting to avoid it is "misguided in the extreme." | "the music I told you **about**" |
| Don't start with *And/But/Or/So/Because* | **IGNORE** | A simplification fed to children so they stop running sentences together — "inappropriate for adults." | "**Because** you're mine, I walk the line." |
| *which* (nonrestrictive) vs. *that* (restrictive) is mandatory | **DEPENDS / mostly IGNORE** | No good reason to ban restrictive *which*; great writers use it ("a day **which** will live in infamy"). The real work is done by **commas**. Following the convention adds polish in formal AmE. | Restrictive *which* OK; comma = nonrestrictive |
| Use *whom*, not *who*, for objects | **DEPENDS** | *whom* is dying; using it can sound pompous or invite hypercorrection. When unsure, *who* is unobjectionable. | "**Who** did you give it to?" |
| Singular *they* is wrong | **IGNORE — Pinker DEFENDS** | Not plural; a generic/bound variable ("Everyone returned to **their** seats" = ∀x, x returned to x's seat). Causes ~zero comprehension delay; generic *he* slows readers. A *return* to older norms. | "If anyone calls, tell **them** I'm out." |
| *less* vs. *fewer* is strict | **DEPENDS / overblown** | *less* is fine with measurement, money, time, distance. "10 items or less" is defensible. Enforce *fewer* only for plain count nouns where it aids clarity. | "**less** than 10 miles," "**less** than \$20" |
| *very unique* is illogical | **IGNORE (aesthetic caveat)** | Uniqueness is relative to which respects you compare; degree is meaningful. Avoid only on stylistic grounds. | defensible, though "distinctive" often reads better |
| Don't use sentence-adverb *Hopefully* | **IGNORE** | Pure folklore. Sentence adverbs (*frankly, mercifully, hopefully*) are a normal class. | "**Hopefully**, the treaty will pass." |
| *like* must join nouns; use *as/as if* for clauses | **IGNORE** | "Doesn't see the point of that one." Far more leeway than sticklers claim. | "It looks **like** it's going to rain." |
| Causal *since* / contrastive *while* are wrong | **IGNORE** | Both have centuries of causal/concessive use. | "**Since** you asked…"; "**While** I agree, …" |
| *between you and I* / pronoun case after *and* | **DEPENDS** | Many speakers have a settled rule allowing nominative after *and*. Formal writing still prefers "between you and **me**." | Formal: "between you and **me**." |
| *none* is always singular | **IGNORE** | A 19th-c. myth. Use notional agreement: singular if it feels singular, plural if plural. | "**None** of the files **were** corrupted." |
| Two-vs-more-than-two pedantry (*between/among*, superlative-of-two) | **IGNORE** | Old chestnuts with no real basis. | "**between** the three of us" |
| No flat adverbs (*drive fast*, *go slow*) | **IGNORE** | Historically native and correct. | "Drive **safe**." |
| Never use the passive voice | **IGNORE (use judiciously)** | A legitimate tool — keep topic in subject position, background an irrelevant agent. | "Mistakes **were made**" when the agent is irrelevant |
| *irregardless* | **IGNORE the battle** | Misuse is now rare; "purists should declare victory and move on." | — |

## Real rules worth keeping — KEEP (flag violations)

| Rule | Why it's real | Bad → Good |
|---|---|---|
| Fix **dangling modifiers** *when they cause real ambiguity* | The problem is ambiguity, not ungrammaticality. Harmless danglers are fine; flag ones that misattach the subject. | "Checking in, **it** was nice to see classmates" → "Checking in, **I** was glad to see classmates." |
| **Subject–verb agreement** across intervening phrases | Agreement-attraction errors (verb agrees with a nearby noun, not the head) misread. | "The **list** of items **are**…" → "…**is**…" |
| ***its* vs. *it's*** | Pure clarity/credibility; errors signal carelessness. | "**It's** color" → "**Its** color." |
| **Pronoun–antecedent clarity** | Reader must always know what a pronoun refers to; ambiguous *it/this/they* breaks comprehension. | "The cable hit the pipe and **it** broke" → name the referent. |
| **Parallel structure** in coordination/lists | Non-parallelism jars and obscures the relationship. | "to hike, swimming, and a nap" → "to hike, swim, and nap." |
| **Commonly confused / malapropped words** | Nonstandard senses defeat communication. | *lie/lay*, *affect/effect*, *flout/flaunt*, *principal/principle* |
| ***literally*** (reserve for non-figurative) | The strictly-speaking sense is indispensable and has no equivalent; misuse signals "I don't care what my words mean." | "I **literally** died" → "I almost died." |
| ***decimate*** etc. — don't use a word in a sense your reader won't recognize | Precision serves communication. | use when "devastate most of" is meant and clear |

> Note the asymmetry: *literally* and *decimate* are KEEP because the distinction
> preserves a uniquely useful meaning — the inverse of *very unique* and
> *irregardless*, where the "distinction" preserves nothing.

## The decision procedure

When any usage dispute arises, resolve in this order:
1. **Clarity first.** Does one option help the reader parse meaning
   faster/unambiguously? Pick it.
2. **Consult data, not dogma.** What do skilled writers + usage panels/dictionaries
   do? A "rule" no good writer follows is folklore.
3. **Diagnose the rule's pedigree.** Latin-imitation, schoolroom simplification, or
   a named peever ⇒ likely bubbe-meise ⇒ IGNORE. Comprehension-grounded ⇒ KEEP.
4. **Know your audience / register.** In formal or sticklerish contexts, observe
   even a superstition (avoid stranded prepositions, write "between you and me") to
   avoid distracting readers — a strategic, not moral, choice.
5. **Keep perspective.** Usage minutiae are the smallest part of good writing.
   Coherence, classic style, and beating the curse of knowledge matter far more.

## One-liners for a rules engine

- **Never flag:** split infinitives, terminal prepositions, sentence-initial
  conjunctions, singular *they*, restrictive *which*, *who* for *whom*,
  sentence-adverb *hopefully*, flat adverbs, *less* with measurements, passive
  voice, *none*-as-plural, *between* for >2.
- **Always flag:** *it's/its* errors, broken parallelism, subject–verb agreement
  attraction, ambiguous pronoun reference, *literally* = figuratively, true
  malaprops (*lie/lay*, *flout/flaunt*).
- **Flag with judgment (DEPENDS):** dangling modifiers (only if ambiguous), *whom*
  (formality), *fewer/less* (true count nouns), *which/that* (formal AmE), *between
  you and me* (formal register).
